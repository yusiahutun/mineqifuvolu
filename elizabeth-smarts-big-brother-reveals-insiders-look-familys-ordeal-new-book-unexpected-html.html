Elizabeth Smart's 'big brother' reveals insider's look of family's ordeal in new book Unexpected

February 2024 Â· 13 minute read

Harrowing new details about the kidnapping of Elizabeth Smart and her family's heart-breaking struggles during the nine months she was missing have been laid bare in a soon-to-be-released book.

Chris Thomas, who Elizabeth describes as 'like a big brother,' was the Smarts' publicist after she was kidnapped at knifepoint from her Salt Lake City home in 2002 aged 14.

He has revealed what it was like behind-the-scenes during the media circus that unfolded around the search for the teen in a case that captured the nation's interest.

In his book, Chris also details seeing Elizabeth for the first time, dressed in a gray blouse made from 'bedsheets' with a 'frayed rope' as her belt, after her rescue and how he and her family grappled with the intense public scrutiny, which saw them fending off accusations a relative was involved in her kidnapping, in an echo of the JonBenet Ramsey case.

Elizabeth Smart poses with her parents, Ed and Lois, in Salt Lake City in 2004

Chris Thomas shared this never-before-seen photo of him and wife Laura with Elizabeth on her wedding in 2012

His involvement in Elizabeth's case has led to a 20-year-long friendship between the pair, which she speaks about with fondness in the foreword of his book, Unexpected: The Backstory Of Finding Elizabeth Smart And Growing Up In The Culture Of An American Religion.

'Chris has been a friend, mentor, and sounding board, like a big brother,' Elizabeth writes.

'He has been a part of or supported me in every major life decision and event I have experienced since my rescue.

'He is included in my "favorites" phone list. He has seen me at my best and my worst, and still hasn't washed his hands of me.'

Ahead of the book's release on March 7, which is just days before the 20th anniversary of Elizabeth's rescue, Chris has also shared never-before-seen photos of himself with the Smarts on their Hawaii holiday about 10 days after her return and on her wedding day.

 

Chris sees Elizabeth for the first time in 'gray blouse made from bedsheets' after she's rescued as she reunites with family

In his book, Chris, who managed the family's media communications but had never met Elizabeth before she went missing, shares the first time he saw the teen after she had been rescued at the police station.

'She has puffy, sunburned cheeks, tightly braided blonde pigtails, and is wearing a gray blouse that looks like it was sewn from bedsheets,' he wrote.

'A frayed rope serves as a belt, holding up her dirty, heavily worn, oversized jeans.'

He also describes how Elizabeth's father Ed 'flew into an absolute rage' over detectives 'interrogating' the teen after she was rescued.

'They can make my life a living hell, but they have no right to do this to my daughter,' Chris quoted Ed as saying.

Elizabeth with her mother (second right) at the Salt Lake City Police Department after she was found alive

He continued: 'A detective tried to calm Ed down by explaining that in order to ensure that the kidnappers... were convicted, investigators had to get as much information as possible from Elizabeth before her answers could be tainted by family, friends, and the media.

'This only infuriated Ed more, and he had to be restrained by several officers.'

After being away from home for many months, the communications professional also shared the moment she was reunited with her siblings.

'All of her siblings except four-year-old William immediately embrace her,' Chris said.

Elizabeth wrote the foreword for Unexpected, which will be released on March 7

'William doesn't recognize Elizabeth and is apprehensive of the roughly dressed stranger, so he recoils and hugs Lois' leg. This draws giggles from Andrew, Mary Katherine, and Edward Junior. Elizabeth seems unsure how to respond.'

The siblings talk among themselves for a few more minutes before the Smarts are escorted out by police into the elevator.

'Once the door closes, Andrew, who is wearing a blue polo shirt and has a thick bowl cut, begins bragging to Elizabeth that he got straight As on his last report card,' Chris wrote.

'"Did you cheat or something?" she responds. "Or did the teachers give you extra credit because I was missing?" She cracks a smile as she elbows Andrew. 

"This is the first time I've heard her speak. I thought she might look tormented and as if she were suffering, but Elizabeth is disarming, funny, and a little sassy.'

 

Grueling 20-hour days as Smart family's publicist while Elizabeth was gone for months

Chris also tells of the grueling schedule, at times 20-hour-plus days, his team and Elizabeth's family undertook as they tried to maintain momentum to keep media attention on the teen's search.

He said his day would start at 3.30am when they would liaise with network and cable shows before prepping Elizabeth's parents Ed and Lois as well as other family members from 4.30am for their interviews, which were scheduled to start from 5am until 10am.

Then they would regroup for an 11am press conference with authorities and this would be followed by an hour of one-on-one interviews with print media.

'This overlaps with interviews with the twenty-four-hour cable networks, which go live every fifteen to twenty minutes,' Chris explained.

She has sat down with Nightline anchor Juju Chang for an interview with Chris

'Each cable network provides the opportunity for dozens of interviews throughout the day. Additional local and network radio and television appearances are also happening simultaneously, leading up to a second daily press conference with law enforcement at 4 p.m. 

'The interviews then continue throughout most of the afternoon and evening, ending around 10:30 p.m.'

After reviewing their day and laying out the game plan for the next day, he and his exhausted team would get less than three hours sleep before doing it all over again.

The relentless routine eventually led to Elizabeth's grandfather Charles banning anyone part on the Smart team from going 'in front of the media if they have not slept for at least four of the previous twenty-four hours' after a particularly harried appearance by her uncle, Tom, in front of a scrum of journalists.

While describing his mindset in his own book, Tom himself drew on a comparison made by a New York Times journalist who 'likened him to Al Pacino's character in the movie Insomnia.' The actor's sleep-deprived cop becomes 'maniacal' as he is obsessed with solving a murder.

 

How media coverage of Elizabeth's disappearance echoed that of JonBenet Ramsey case

In his book, Chris shares insights into how the family reacted when media published a story accusing a family member of being involved in Elizabeth's kidnapping.

He said the story conjured up parallels between the search for Elizabeth and the JonBenet Ramsey case, recalling when 'fingers started pointing at the [child beauty queen's] family, they went into hiding and sent their attorney to respond to the media.' Chris recalls thinking the Smarts needed to 'do the opposite.'

Chris quotes from the story: 'Evidence from within Elizabeth Smart's Salt Lake City home has led investigators to theorize the girl may have been abducted by a member of her extended family, who staged it to look like the work of an outsider, the Salt Lake Tribune has learned.

Brian David Mitchell (left) was sentenced to life in prison for his crimes, but Wanda Barzee (right) was given just 15 years

Elizabeth pictured her family in 2004 at their Salt Lake City home after she was rescued

'Detectives have been unable to explain how the abductor - seen only by the younger girl [Elizabeth's sister Mary Katherine] - could have entered the house through the small window that appeared to be the entry point, according to sources in four law enforcement agencies. The window isn't in the girls' bedroom, but police have refused to specify where it is located.

'Investigators have surmised that someone who was already inside the home may have tried to make the window look as though a break-in occurred there. But the screen appears to have been cut from the inside.'

Upon hearing the story, Elizabeth's aunt Cynthia tells him: 'Chris, you may not know our family well, but I can assure you there is no truth to this. There is no way anyone in our family could have done this. No way, no one, none at all.'

The family decided to rebut the story by releasing a statement, which said: 'The Smart family is disappointed by today's Salt Lake Tribune article, which is highly speculative and implies that it is unusual to investigate the family in this type of case.

'Investigating the family is common procedure. We continue to fully cooperate in every aspect of the investigation and urge the public and media to avoid distraction from what is most important, finding Elizabeth.'

 

False allegations of a family sex ring after nude images of adult males were found on Ed's computer

During her disappearance, news leaked that materials found on Ed's computer included some images of nude adult males, Chris wrote. Ed is pictured here with then wife Lois in 2002

But these accusations of a family member being involved weren't the most controversial that the Smarts had to endure. 

Soon news leaked that materials found on Ed's computer included 'some images of nude adult males,' Chris wrote.

'Complying with the requests of investigators, the Smarts surrendered all of their electronic devices in the hours following the kidnapping,' he said.

'Despite finding nothing of consequence on the machines, someone in the Salt Lake City Police Department leaked these private details, and now the National Enquirer is, according to several reputable journalists, about to break a big story.'

Before the National Enquirer went to print, Chris said he was asked by journalists about Ed's sexuality, what was found on his computer, his marriage and 'other salacious questions.'

Eventually a story is published but it's 'botched,' and falsely accuses Ed and his brothers of being in 'involved in a gay sex ring that led to Elizabeth's kidnapping.'

In 2003, the National Enquirer apologized to the family for the 'inaccurate and false' story, according to The Advocate, after a settlement was reached by the parties.

The National Enquirer article was published weeks after Elizabeth was abducted and led to the firing of two Salt Lake Tribune reporters who were paid $20,000 for their part in the story.

 

Family's frustration over police handling of search for Elizabeth  

Chris and Elizabeth with Wolf Blitzer after she released her own book, My Story, in 2013

Chris said he and some family members were continuously frustrated by the police's obsession with the wrong suspect, and they dragged their feet about releasing the sketch of the man who was eventually found guilty of kidnapping Elizabeth.

For months, police wrongly suspected Richard Ricci, an ex-convict and career criminal, was responsible for Elizabeth's abduction despite the only witness, sister Mary Katherine, insisting it wasn't him.

He revealed details about the moment Mary Katherine, who was nine at the time of her sister's disappearance, remembered who had taken Elizabeth.

'Mary Katherine had been in her room, reading the Guinness Book of World Records. For months she had been trying to match in her mind the voice of the abductor with someone she had previously met,' Chris wrote.

Chris's book release comes just days before the 20th anniversary of Elizabeth's rescue

Ed told him Mary Katherine said it was 'this homeless man Lois met downtown, who worked on the roof one afternoon.' 

But Chris said police were 'surprisingly' uninterested in the revelation.

He also wrote about an attempted break-in at the house of Elizabeth's cousin's Olivia Wright that was carried out by the real kidnapper, and how he wished he had confirmed the story to a reporter when he was asked about it at the time.

'The attempted break-in on July 24, like several other developments in the coming months, contradicts Ricci being the perpetrator, and yet law enforcement always seems to quickly dismiss contradictions like this,' Chris said.

'For years I will wonder how things might have been different if I had allowed the news to break that day.'

After being publicly criticized by the Smart family, Police Chief Rick Dinse acknowledged investigators had been slow to release the composite of Brian David Mitchell, which was done with Mary Katherine's input.

'Hindsight is 20-20 vision. If we had to go back over it again, I think every one of [our investigators] would say, "I wish we had gone public with thatâ¦ earlier,"' Dinse said in 2003, according to Associated Press.

 

How Elizabeth's mother Lois said emotional 'goodbye' to her daughter months after she went missing

As the search for Elizabeth dragged on, Lois 'bravely let go' of her daughter by saying a 'very personal goodbye' so she could focus on the needs of her other children.

'A couple of months after the abduction, Lois could not go on any longer with the thought that someone was holding her daughter captive,' Chris said.

'In order to have the strength and focus to attend to the needs of her other five children, she bravely let go.'

As the search for Elizabeth dragged on, Lois 'bravely let go' of her daughter by saying a 'very personal goodbye.' The Smarts are pictured with their daughter in 2003

Lois took an early-morning horse ride with Elizabeth's boots, spurs, gloves and cowboy hat to one of the teen's favorite locations: Utah's Moffit Peak.

'She paused, peered out at the mountain vista, and began to cry,' Chris wrote.

'Lois then removed from her vest the light blue circular pin that had Elizabeth's picture on it and the words, "Pray for Me," and deposited it into the earth.

'After more tears and retrospection, she got back on the horse and headed back home, fully focused as a mother of her surviving children.'

 

The incredible moment Chris learned that Elizabeth was alive

Chris recalls getting a mysterious call from Ed who had defied the police's orders by calling him.

'I'm afraid I'm going to have to miss our meeting, and I know we have a lot of work to do to prepare for the press conference,' Ed told him.

'I was told to go to the Sandy City Police Department. Not to stop. Not to talk to anyone, not even Lois.'

After hanging up with Ed, Chris called a detective and close friend who worked with Sandy City Police Department and after several attempts, he finally gets through.

It had been the news he had been waiting months for, his friend tells him: 'We've brought in an indigent teenager who we think is Elizabeth Smart. We're waiting for Ed to come and identify her.'

Chris and wife Laura (back) with the Smarts and Elizabeth (front right) on their Hawaiian holiday about 10 days after her rescue

Elizabeth, now 35, is a mother of three. She's pictured with her children and husband Matthew 

At first, Chris assumes they've brought in a body, but the friend is quick to correct him: 'What body? She's sitting in the room next to me. She's alive.'

After Elizabeth's return, the family goes on a holiday arranged by one of Chris's 'very generous clients' for him and wife Laura to take the Smarts to Hawaii.

On the holiday, strangers who had been so familiar with Elizabeth's face on the news failed to recognize her.

'In the ten days since she had been rescued, Elizabeth's appearance had changed. With sleep, exercise, and proper nutrition, she appeared like a more mature version of the girl from the photos and videos that had been flashed across the news while she was missing,' Chris wrote.

'She looked much less like the weathered, puffy-cheeked teenager from the handful of images that had been released since she had returned home.

'Nearly everyone who approached Ed and Lois on the trip didn't recognize Elizabeth, and asked them how she was doing. This usually happened while Elizabeth was standing right in front of them. 

'On a kayaking expedition, the guide even privately asked me who was taking care of Elizabeth while the family was away. When I explained she was in the boat behind us, the guide did a double take and said, "Really? That's her?"'
