A swimming instructor has cautioned parents about the dangers of a popular floating device, detailing the three reasons why the pool floatie can be life-threatening. 

Nikki Scarnati, 32, is an Infant Swimming Recourse (ISR) instructor who frequently shares tips to help keep the little ones safe in the water on her TikTok channel. 

Most recently, the Florida-based coach issued a warning to parents about using floating devices that have a canopy at the top when in the pool with their baby. 

She claimed that the Mambo baby float can increase the risk of drowning because it isn't approved by the US Coast Guard, can flip over, and make it more difficult to rescue your child.

'My take on the Mambo baby float,' she captioned the video before detailing the three reasons you should avoid purchasing the $79 floatation device. 

Nikki began by alleging that the swimming floatie wasn't a good choice because it isn't approved by the US Coast Guard. 

Next, she said she wouldn't recommend the Mambo baby float because it's 'notorious for flipping over.' 

'All it takes is for a child to lean forward a little too much and they're going to flip over.

'And if they do flip over, they're strapped into this device so it's going to be more difficult for you to remove your child and get them poolside so evaluate them,' she said. 

Nikki explained that babies under four are 'very top-heavy,' meaning their 'heads are much bigger than their bodies.' 

'Different postures in the water are going to be detrimental when it comes down to a child's survival, which is why we don't focus on doggy paddle positions below the age of four because they're at a higher risk of getting stuck in the vertical position,' she explained. 

The swimming coach continued: 'If you take a look at a photo of a baby in the Mambo baby float, the little one is laying on her back but her head is propped up because she's looking at people. 

'If she were to fall in the water without that flotation, her body is going to be forced vertically because again her head is much bigger than her body.

'[In another photo], the child is on their belly, and their head is propped up. That is going to force their body into the vertical position because of how these children are anatomically at this age.' 

The swimming instructor that specializes in helping infants learn their way in the water also revealed that the canopy that is over the baby's head is a danger to them. 

She said that this is because if the device flips over, the canopy can become stuck on top of a child's face, making it increasingly more difficult to rescue them. 

At the end of her video, Nikki suggested parents steer away from floating devices and instead hold their baby.

On its website, Mambo Baby insists that there is 'no doubt about the safety of the Mambobaby float', however it notes that the design is 'not a life-saving product' and that it should only ever be used 'under the constant supervision of adults'. 

'It is filled with solid foam like a life jacket and has a patented safety structure. From the chest, shoulders, and crotch of the five-way protection harness protect your baby is stable and safe with Mambobaby float,' the brand's online FAQ section explains. 

The company adds that it is 'working hard' to achieve approval from the US Coast Guard for its product, noting that 'no baby floats suitable for infants under 2 years old have passed USCG [approval]'.

The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has also warned parents to avoid using neck floats specifically for babies because it can increase the risk of drowning.

While Goldfish Swim School has said that pool floaties are 'not a good idea for young children' because there are many 'dangers.' 

According to Stop Drowning Now, drowning is the leading cause of death in children ages one to four and can happen in as little as 20 to 60 seconds.
