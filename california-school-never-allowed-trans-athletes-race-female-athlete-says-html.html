California school should never have allowed trans athletes to take partin race, female athlete say

September 2024 · 5 minute read

A track and field athlete who is suing the state of Connecticut for making her compete against trans athletes in high school says two transgender athletes should have never been allowed to take part in a California championship competition.

Athena Ryan and Lorelei Barrett, two trans athletes who were both born male, qualified for California's women's high school state championships but did not show for the race amid the ongoing debate about their participation and fear for their own well-being.

Chelsea Mitchell, who was once dubbed 'the fastest girl in Connecticut', slammed the decision by California state to allow them to compete alongside those born female.

The former high school racer said it was a disadvantage to the other athletes as they had an 'unfair' biological advantage against their female-born competitors. 

Mitchell herself is set to appeal her case against the state of Connecticut after she sued them for allowing trans athletes to participate in competitive school sports against others of their identified gender.

'None of us asked to have these policies put in place. It shouldn't be up to us athletes to decide these policies,' Mitchell told Fox News.

'At the end of the day, it is unfair these biological males are entering the female category and [that's] why our institutions need to step up and put policies in place that protect the female category.'

Former college swimmer Riley Gaines also hit out at California allowing trans runners to compete in women's' sports.

After both Ryan and Barrett failed to show for the preliminary rounds, Gaines took to Twitter to say that they 'clearly possess an unfair advantage'.

California is one of several states that allows high school athletes to compete in sports according to their gender identity without regulation.

California Interscholastic Federation's 'Gender Identity Participation' policy notes that students are able to compete 'consistent with their gender identity'. 

Protests subsequently broke out amid the event last week where demonstrators were outraged at the inclusion of transgender athletes in girls' sports.

Last Saturday, the Twitter account of one of the protesters, Women Are Real, showed them being chased out by an official for the California Interscholastic Federation State Track & Field Championships.

He then asks them to 'put it away' in reference to the person filming. The Women Are Real account claims that their banners were then confiscated.

The CIF condemned those protesting for what they called 'harassing behaviors' that they claim led Ryan and one other transgender athlete - Lorelei Barrett of Sherman Oaks Buckley - to not show up.

'The CIF is disappointed for two of our student-athletes and their families because due to the actions of others, they found it necessary to withdraw from the State Track and Field Championships out of concern for the student's well being,' they told the LA Times.

'The CIF strongly denounces discriminatory or harassing behaviors that impact our student-athletes' opportunities to participate in interscholastic competitions.'

In 2020, Mitchell launched, along with two other runners, a lawsuit seeking to block a state policy that allowed high school athletes to compete based on the gender with which they identify, arguing transgender girls have an unfair physical advantage.

The case was subsequently dismissed by a federal judge in Connecticut in April 2021, but they have since appealed the decision.

The initial lawsuit centered on two trans sprinters, Terry Miller and Andraya Yearwood, who frequently outperformed their female-born competitors. 

The two seniors combined to win 15 girls state indoor or outdoor championship races since 2017, the 2020 lawsuit claimed.

Mitchell is about to head back to court to appeal the case challenging the ruling that allowed trans students to participate in competitive sports for their identified gender.
